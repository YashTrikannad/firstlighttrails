The Imp is one of those rare White Mountain hikes that punches well above its elevation. At 3,615 feet, it sits comfortably in the "52 With a View" list rather than the 4,000-footer crowd — but the open ledges and cliffside views feel every bit as rewarding as peaks twice its prominence.

The trail's namesake is the "Imp" profile: a weathered rock face on the north side of the ridge that, when viewed from Route 16 in the valley below, resembles a mischievous elf looking down over the highway. It's one of those geological coincidences that's easy to miss until someone points it out, then impossible to unsee.

## The Approach

You can do it either as a loop or an out and back. You can start from the north imp trailhead on Route 16 in Gorham. The trail enters hardwood forest immediately, ascending steadily through birch and yellow birch that put on a remarkable show in mid-October. The gradient is consistent — never brutally steep, but it doesn't let up either except for a quarter mile in the middle.

About two miles in, the forest opens up and you'll hit the open ledges. This is where the hike earns its place on the 52WAV list. The ledges face west, and on a clear day you get an unobstructed panorama of the Presidential Range: Madison, Adams, Jefferson, and Washington lined up across the valley.

## The Summit Ledges

The true summit of Imp Face is a series of open ledges rather than a single high point. The cliffside drops away sharply to the east, and you can walk along the rim to find your preferred viewpoint. When I did this hike for a sunrise during a lunar eclipse event, I was the only one on the ledges in front of the panoramic views of the preseidentials. It is an amazing spot to see the first rays of light hit the high peaks however note that if you want the view of the sunrise itself, there is none to be had from here as the carter range will be blocking your view eastwards. Same would be true for sunset as well although you might see more color in the sky, the presidential range is very high so the sun would set behind them pretty soon!

## The Loop Back

The south return leg descends through denser spruce-fir forest, quieter and less-traveled than the north approach. The grade is steeper on this side, requiring some care on wet rock. It rejoins Route 16 about a mile south of the north trailhead — plan for a short road walk back to the parking area, or spot a second car.

## Conditions & Notes

The open ledges can be exposed and slick when wet. The north approach dries faster than the south descent. In October, this is one of the better foliage hikes in the region — the birch and maple on the lower slopes peak around the second week of the month.

The trailhead parking on Route 16 is small (6-8 cars). Arrive early on fall weekends or use the Glen Ellis parking area a mile south as overflow.
